Welcome! I’m Summiya Najam, an economist broadly interested in studying development and behavior. Currently, I serve as a Research Fellow at the Stanford Institute for Economic Policy Research (SIEPR), where I collaborate with Prof. Alessandra Voena on projects that delve into the intersection of development, labor, and gender economics.

Before joining Stanford, I earned my MSc with Distinction at the University of Oxford. Prior to that, I studied Economics at Williams College, nestled in the charming Berkshires.

I was raised in Islamabad, Pakistan, a city whose vibrant culture and spirit I cherish every day. In my leisure time, you’ll find me delving into Urdu poetry, enjoying the soulful rhythms of South Asian music, or cooking recipes that remind me of my mother.

Outside of my professional work, I actively mentor students from marginalized backgrounds, assisting them with their applications to graduate programs. If you're interested in being a mentor, consider joining the Oxford Refugee-led Research Hub, a project I’ve recently collaborated with to support displaced students in applying for graduate studies.

I'd be happy to share my resume if you'd like!

Research Interests

Having spent significant parts of my life immersed in two vastly different cultures, I have always been captivated by the immense influence of social norms, over logical reasoning, in shaping individual preferences and behaviors. Unlike the rational agents often depicted in economic models, I observed that people are rarely driven purely by logic. Instead, their actions are shaped by cultural norms, cognitive biases, and entrenched practices, which collectively determine their social and economic outcomes.

My academic interests lie at the intersection of development and behavioral economics, with a particular focus on how institutional design, social norms, and networks influence critical decisions such as marriage timing, fertility choices, and labor force participation in low-income countries. I aim to integrate insights from psychology to deepen our understanding of these complex determinants. Recently, I read Christina Biccheri's "Norms in the Wild" and highly recommend it to anyone curious about how we can identify, measure, and shape social norms to drive meaningful change.
